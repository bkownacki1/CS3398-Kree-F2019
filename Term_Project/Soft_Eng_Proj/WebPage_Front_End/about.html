About Texas State Parking Apocolypse

Why is this even an issue?

Great question! The thing is, this issue was much worse in previous years; and, even though we all suffer through it now, Parking Services seems to be trying to make some progress towards a solution. This can be seen here. As you may notice, for the Fiscal Year 2018, Parking Services sold just over 20,000 parking permits of all types. This, despite them having just under 12,000 parking spots across the San Marcos and Round Rock Campuses. In previous years, Parking Services sold about 5,000 more Perimeter Permits than in Fiscal Year 2018. This means that, although they are selling fewer Perimeter Permits, they still sell about 70% more permits than they have spots available for. All those spaces need to accomodate almost 45,000 people (about 40,000 Students plus over 4300 Faculty & Staff). That's a huge discrepancy.

Of course not all the available spots will be used at all times, so it doesn't make sense to ensure that a space exists for every single permit, but Parking Services can still do a lot better to provide parking accomadations. For example, there have only been two parking garages or lots added to Texas State since 2005, adding a total of about 1600 parking spaces. This is with Texas State's planning assumptions of 1.5% undergraduate student growth per year (about 600 additional students per year), per their 2017-2027 University Master Plan. In that same Master Plan, the University recognizes that there is a parking shortage, but their solution is to add spaces farther from central campus and provide more busing to the campus core. They also admit that they are placing a priority not on student, staff, or residental parking, but paid visitor parking, "in proximity to high profile public destinations."

What insights can we glean from the university's plan? Well, being that the LBJ Student Center paid parking garage accounts for nearly 20% of Parking Services' yearly income while accounting for just 5% of its yearly expenses, the University seems to be looking to take a model of easy profit over easy scholarship. The fact that the University is prioritizing paid public parking for people who will visit the University a handful of times over permitted Staff/Faculty and Student parking, which is utilized by tens of thousands of people daily, is something we find insulting.

In fact, the Univerity's Master Plan only includes plans for an addtional 2,000 parking spaces from now until 2027. Remember, they also plan to add about 6,000 students in that same time frame. The gains made by these additional parking spaces will be mitigated by the increase in student population and by the fact that in the previous sentence in their master plan, they state that they want to migrate parking away from the campus core to these new facilities. Many of our fellow Civil Engineering Faculty and Students will concur that moving away from single-level surface lots and towards multi-level garages is the correct way to maximize efficiency of space, but where the University gets it wrong is their plan to move this new, more efficient parking farther from where it needs to be.

What's the Solution?

For us the solution is to work around the problem. Since we cannot hope to pursuade the Univerity to add more parking than they plan to (they, somehwat comically, self-assess 77% of parking spaces occupied on a given day during peak periods), we must do the best we can with what we have. That is why we, as the designers of this website and tool, have put so many hours into researching and developing a way for our fellow Students, Faculty, and Staff to take best advantage of the shoddy infrastructure that the University provides. Throughout their Master Plan, the University asserts that there is no war in Ba Sing Se by using flabberghastingly wrong or irrelevant statistics, which many people who regularly visit Texas State can identify as ridiculous. We hope that by utilizing this tool, you will be able to experience a small amount more ease in your commute.

For the University, the solution is to turn many of the surface-level lots in central campus into multi-level garages accessable to students. We also would like to see new garages placed in closer walking distance to campus for all permit holders. Finally, the University needs to upgrade the surface-level commuter lots currently located far from the center of campus into multi-level garages to provide double the current parking capacity. To make Texas State University into a model for accessability and ease-of-use, the University must double the number of spots it currently has available so that anybody who pays for a permit is guaranteed an easy-to-find spot within a reasonable distance of campus. If the university cannot manage that, it must sell fewer permits so those who pay for a spot will have one.
